Antigens with Background data
How Background QC works. Background wells are the plate blanks — wells where no specimen was loaded (Row A / A11 & A12 on the pilot). For each antigen we look at every Background well's MFI and ask three things:
  1. Mean Background MFI. The average non-specific signal for that antigen. A useful baseline; not flagged on its own.
  2. %CV across Background wells. Standard deviation divided by the mean, expressed as a percentage. Measures how consistent the background is well-to-well. If %CV is high, the antigen's background signal is noisy and any downstream specimen result for that antigen is less trustworthy. Antigens are flagged CV when %CV exceeds {{ bg_cv_pct }}% (editable on the Settings page).
  3. Max Background MFI. The single highest MFI seen in any Background well for that antigen. A high value suggests contamination, carry-over, or cross-reactivity from the bead itself. Antigens are flagged MAX when any Background well exceeds {{ bg_max_mfi }} MFI (editable on the Settings page).
Antigens with neither flag get OK. These three numbers mirror the legacy MPOX NC-bead checks, applied to the Background wells whenever the plate has no named negative control. Excluded analytes are still listed (muted) so they don't silently disappear from the QC trail.

What does "Fit OK" mean? Each antigen's standard points are fit to a 4-parameter logistic (4PL) curve. A fit is marked OK only when all four of these criteria pass:
  • R² ≥ 0.95 on log10(MFI) — the curve actually tracks the data.
  • IC50 inside the tested dilution range (with ±3× margin) — the inflection isn't off the side of the curve.
  • Hill slope between 0.3 and 5.0 — the curve isn't suspiciously flat or step-like.
  • Dynamic range ≥ 3× — upper-plateau MFI is at least 3× the lower-plateau MFI, so there's real signal to fit.
A fit that converges but fails any of these is marked FAIL; the parameters are still shown, but the LLOQ / ULOQ and %-in-range are unreliable. Excluded analytes are not used for laboratory decisions even if the fit passes.

One row per antigen. %-in-range is the fraction of specimens whose MFI falls between the antigen's LLOQ and ULOQ on the 4PL.

Negative Control QC
How Negative Control QC works. A negative control (NC) is a well containing a sample known to be antibody-negative for every antigen on the panel — typically a pooled seronegative serum. On a working plate the NC's MFI should sit near zero for every antigen; any antigen that lights up in the NC is a flag for cross-reactivity in the assay, contamination of the NC sample, or a problem with the bead region itself.
  • NC wells are identified by sample name matching NC* or Negative* (editable on the Settings page).
  • The heatmap below shows the MFI of every NC well × antigen pair. Darker purple = higher MFI. A clean NC reads uniformly pale; isolated dark cells are the antigens to investigate.
  • NCs are distinct from Background wells (the plate blanks in Row A). When the plate has no NC, the Background QC section above is the closest substitute — but it can't catch sample-driven issues, only buffer / bead issues.
  • NC MFI is persisted to a cross-plate history JSON, so when you accumulate multiple plates the historical heatmap below surfaces drift over time.
